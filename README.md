## Overview

Yamata no Orochi (八岐大蛇, 'eight-branching great serpent') is the most famous monster of Japanese mythology, recorded in Japan's two oldest written texts: the Kojiki (712 CE) and the Nihon Shoki (720 CE). According to the Kojiki, the serpent had eight heads and eight tails, eyes like red cherries, a body covered in moss, cypress, and cedar, spanning eight valleys and eight peaks, with a belly perpetually oozing blood. The storm deity Susanoo no Mikoto encountered the grieving couple Ashinazuchi and Tenazuchi, who had lost seven daughters to the serpent's annual appetite; only Kushinada-hime remained. Susanoo defeated the creature through stratagem: he transformed Kushinada-hime into a comb hidden in his hair, instructed the parents to brew eightfold-refined sake in eight vessels, and hid these behind a fence. The Orochi, discovering the sake, drank from each vessel with one of its heads and fell into a drunken stupor. Susanoo then cut the creature apart with his Totsuka-no-Tsurugi sword. When he severed the middle tail, his blade met resistance and broke — revealing the legendary Kusanagi-no-Tsurugi ('Grass-Cutting Sword') hidden within the serpent's flesh. This sword was subsequently presented to Amaterasu, the sun goddess, and became one of Japan's three Imperial Regalia. Scholars have proposed that the Orochi myth encodes historical realities: the serpent may represent the Hi River's flooding, the narrative may reflect ancient conflict between Izumo Province culture and the encroaching Yamato state, and the metallurgical theme (the sword emerging from the serpent) may reflect the spread of iron-smelting technology from the Korean peninsula.

- **Origin**: Old Japanese; documented in the Kojiki (712 CE)
- **Meaning**: Yamata (八岐/八俣): eight-forked or eight-branched. No: possessive or descriptive particle. Orochi: great serpent, from Old Japanese woröti; possibly combining ō (great) with chi (god/spirit) or dialectal oro (peak/tail).
- **First use**: 712 CE (Kojiki); the myth predates the written record in oral tradition

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Hydrological / river-flood encoding** _(Miura Sukeyuki)_

Scholars including Miura Sukeyuki propose that Yamata no Orochi personifies the Hi River in flood, which annually devastated the valley. The eight heads represent the river's tributaries or multiple flood channels; Susanoo's defeat of the serpent represents human mastery of water management and flood control.

**Metallurgical / iron-technology encoding** _(Scholars of Japanese metallurgical history)_

Alternative analysis proposes the narrative reflects the introduction of iron-smelting from the Korean peninsula. The serpent represents a mountain spirit controlling iron-sand-bearing flood deposits; Susanoo (associated with the iron-producing area of Susa in Iishi District) defeats it, and the sword emerging from the creature symbolizes the mastery of iron technology. The Nita District nearby is documented as an ancient iron-production center.

**Political conflict encoding (Izumo vs. Yamato)** _(Historians of ancient Japanese political consolidation)_

The myth may encode historical conflict between the politically distinct Izumo culture and the encroaching Yamato imperial state. Susanoo, exiled from the heavenly realm, arrives in Izumo and kills its most powerful creature — a possible mythological representation of Yamato political and military expansion over the Izumo region.

**Comparative dragon-slayer mythology** _(Ōbayashi Taryō)_

Scholar Ōbayashi Taryō links the Orochi myth to a widespread cross-cultural dragon-slayer archetype, including Perseus and Cetus in Greek mythology, suggesting possible transmission through southern Korea or the Yangzi River cultural corridor.
